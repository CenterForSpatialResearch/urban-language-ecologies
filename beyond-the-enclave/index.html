Beyond the Enclave: Languages of New York

New York City is one of the most linguistically diverse places in the world; linguists estimate that more than 800 different languages are spoken in the five boroughs.

And yet, we often think of language in a city like of New York as centered around “ethnic enclaves,” neighborhoods where minority language speakers cluster. While that model may hold some truth, it obscures how deeply integrated multilingualism is into the fabric of city life. This project offers a snapshot into some of the ways language is organized in the city - beyond the enclave.

Next Fifty Languages

The Queens Public Library collection contains materials in over 200 languages. Though the holdings in each library reflect the makeup of the neighborhood, patrons have access to all of it. The diversity of the collection is best understood by looking at the books in the middle. The 5 largest (Chinese, Spanish, Korean, Russian, and French) and the 150 smallest collections have been removed to illustrate the true diversity of languages available, and what we find is a collection that connects the world.

Multilingual (UN) Building

Many buildings in New York City seem like a miniature United Nations, where speakers of different and often unrelated languages live very close together. In this building, some apartments are connected by family (Polish, Patois, and Russian), but many are not, instead connected only by the walls they share and the lingua franca they use to bridge the communicative and cultural divide.

Street Festival

Almost every weekend in the summer, there is a street festival in New York City. Though many are centered around one ethnographic-linguistic group or geographic region, they take on a multilingual, multicultural identity through the attendees. Sometimes in a lingua franca, sometimes in a local language, and sometimes in another language altogether, these events bring together widely diverse communities.

Food Trucks

Across the globe, food is associated with language, and in diasporic communities, culinary traditions often outlast linguistic ones. In a city such as New York, food trucks gather on corners, in business districts, and near schools. And even though customers may not always speak the language of the food they eat, workers don’t always speak it either.

Bilingual Building

Though a building might not be an ethnic enclave against the backdrop of the city, but for the residents, it can be an oasis. Buildings such as this one form micro-communities that have the power to sustain familial, linguistic, and social ties. Most of the Polish speaking residents are related by blood, marriage, or friendship, and all have at least one connection to Poland.
